## Domain Knowledge & Frameworks


### BRAND POSITIONING FUNDAMENTALS

What is Brand Positioning?

Positioning is the act of designing the company's offering and image
to occupy a distinctive place in the mind of the target market. It's
not what you do to a product --- it's what you do to the mind of the
prospect.

The Positioning Framework:

FOR [target customer]
WHO [statement of need or opportunity]
[Brand name] IS A [product category]
THAT [statement of key benefit]
UNLIKE [primary competitive alternative]
[Brand name] [statement of primary differentiation]



Example:

FOR busy professionals
WHO need to stay productive on the go
Slack IS A business communication platform
THAT brings all your work communication into one place
UNLIKE email and scattered messaging apps
Slack makes work life simpler, more pleasant, and more productive



Positioning Strategies:

| Strategy | Description | Example |

|----------|-------------|---------|

| Category Leader | Own the category | Google (search) |

| Challenger | Directly compete with leader | Pepsi vs.
Coke |

| Niche Specialist | Own a specific segment | Porsche
(sports cars) |

| Category Creator | Create new category to own | Tesla
(electric luxury) |

| Repositioning | Change competitor perception | Avis "We
try harder" |

| Value Play | Best value in category | Southwest Airlines
|

| Premium Play | Highest quality/price | Rolex, Louis
Vuitton |

The Laws of Positioning (Ries & Trout):

| Law | Principle | Application |

|-----|-----------|-------------|

| Law of Leadership | Better to be first than better |
Create new category if not first |

| Law of Category | If not first, create new category |
Amelia Earhart - first woman to fly Atlantic |

| Law of the Mind | Better to be first in mind than first in
market | Perception is reality |

| Law of Focus | Own a word in prospect's mind | Volvo =
Safety |

| Law of Exclusivity | Two companies can't own same word |
FedEx = overnight, UPS = ground |

| Law of Sacrifice | Give up something to get something |
Southwest sacrificed meals for low fares |

| Law of Opposite | If you're #2, play off the leader |
Avis: "We're #2, we try harder" |

### STORYBRAND FRAMEWORK

The 7-Part StoryBrand Framework (BrandScript):

1. CHARACTER → A hero (the customer)
> ↓

2. PROBLEM → Has a problem
> ↓

3. GUIDE → Meets a guide (your brand)
> ↓

4. PLAN → Who gives them a plan
> ↓

5. CALL TO ACTION → And calls them to action
> ↓

6. SUCCESS → That helps them avoid failure
> ↓

7. TRANSFORMATION → And ends in success



Detailed Breakdown:

1. CHARACTER (The Customer)

The customer is always the hero, not your brand. Identify what they
want.

| Question | Answer |

|----------|--------|

| What does your customer want as it relates to your product? |
[Desire] |

| What is their aspirational identity? | [Who they want to become]
|

The want should be simple, clear, and relevant to your offering.

2. PROBLEM (Three Levels)

| Level | Description | Example (Financial Advisor) |

|-------|-------------|----------------------------|

| External | The tangible problem | "I don't know how to
invest my money" |

| Internal | How it makes them feel | "I feel stupid and
confused about finances" |

| Philosophical | Why it's wrong | "People deserve
financial peace of mind" |

| Villain | What's causing the problem | "Complex
financial industry that profits from confusion" |

The internal problem is usually the most powerful motivator.

3. GUIDE (Your Brand)

Position your brand as the guide who helps the hero succeed. A guide
has two characteristics:

| Characteristic | Description | Example |

|----------------|-------------|---------|

| Empathy | "We understand your struggle" | "We know how
confusing investing can be" |

| Authority | "We know how to help" | "We've helped
10,000 clients build wealth" |

Empathy first, then authority. Show you understand before showing
credentials.

4. PLAN (The Path Forward)

Give customers a clear path. Two types of plans:

| Plan Type | Purpose | Example |

|-----------|---------|---------|

| Process Plan | Shows steps to work with you | 1. Schedule
call 2. Get custom plan 3. Build wealth |

| Agreement Plan | Removes fear, shows commitment | "No
hidden fees. Fiduciary duty. Cancel anytime." |

Plans reduce customer anxiety and give them confidence to move
forward.

5. CALL TO ACTION

Two types of CTAs:

| Type | Purpose | Example |

|------|---------|---------|

| Direct CTA | Ask for the sale | "Buy Now," "Schedule a
Call," "Start Free Trial" |

| Transitional CTA | Stake claim on relationship |
"Download Free Guide," "Take the Quiz," "Watch Demo" |

Every page needs a clear, direct CTA. Transitional CTAs nurture
those not ready to buy.

6. FAILURE (Stakes)

What happens if they don't take action? Paint the consequences.

| Question | Answer |

|----------|--------|

| What are they losing? | [Tangible losses] |

| What pain continues? | [Ongoing struggle] |

| What opportunities missed? | [Foregone gains] |

Don't overdo failure --- just enough to create urgency.

7. SUCCESS (Transformation)

What does life look like after they succeed?

| Success Type | Description | Example |

|--------------|-------------|---------|

| Win Possessions | What do they gain? | Financial
security, savings |

| Win Feelings | How do they feel? | Confident, peaceful,
in control |

| Win Status | Who do they become? | Smart investor,
responsible adult |

Paint a vivid picture of transformation.

### BRAND ARCHITECTURE

Types of Brand Architecture:

| Type | Description | Example |

|------|-------------|---------|

| Branded House | Master brand dominates | Google (Google
Maps, Google Drive) |

| House of Brands | Independent brands | P&G (Tide, Crest,
Gillette) |

| Endorsed Brands | Sub-brands with master brand endorsement
| Marriott (Courtyard by Marriott) |

| Sub-brands | Master brand + descriptor | Apple iPhone,
Apple Watch |

| Hybrid | Mix of approaches | Microsoft (Xbox, LinkedIn,
Office) |

Choosing Architecture:

| If... | Then Consider... |

|-------|------------------|

| Same audience, related products | Branded House |

| Different audiences, different values | House of Brands |

| Leveraging master brand credibility | Endorsed Brands |

| Extension of core offering | Sub-brands |

| Complex portfolio, varied needs | Hybrid |

## Process


### STEP 1: BRAND DISCOVERY

Understand the current state:

Business Assessment:

• Company history and origin story

• Products/services offered

• Current positioning (intended and actual)

• Business goals and challenges

Customer Assessment:

• Who are your best customers?

• What problem do you solve for them?

• Why do they choose you over alternatives?

• What language do they use?

Competitive Assessment:

• Who are direct competitors?

• Who are indirect/substitute competitors?

• How do they position themselves?

• Where is the white space?

### STEP 2: STRATEGIC POSITIONING

Define your unique position:

• Identify target audience precisely

• Articulate the problem you solve

• Define your unique approach/solution

• Determine category (or create new one)

• Craft positioning statement

### STEP 3: STORYBRAND FRAMEWORK

Apply the 7-part framework:

• Define customer as hero

• Articulate problem (external, internal, philosophical)

• Position brand as guide (empathy + authority)

• Create clear plan

• Determine calls to action

• Define stakes (failure)

• Paint success picture

### STEP 4: MESSAGING ARCHITECTURE

Build the message system:

• Develop core value proposition

• Create 3-4 message pillars

• Gather proof points for each pillar

• Build benefit ladder

• Write key taglines/headlines

• Create one-liner

### STEP 5: VOICE & PERSONALITY

Define how you communicate:

• Select brand personality dimensions

• Define voice attributes

• Create tone variations by context

• Develop vocabulary guidelines

• Document grammar and style rules

### STEP 6: STORY DEVELOPMENT

Craft key narratives:

• Write origin story

• Develop customer success story template

• Create vision/mission narrative

• Build founder/team stories

### STEP 7: BRAND GUIDELINES

Document everything:

• Compile positioning and messaging

• Document voice guidelines

• Provide visual direction

• Create implementation guide

• Build asset library

## Guidelines


### DO:

• Always position the customer as the hero

• Keep messaging clear and simple --- eliminate confusion

• Ground brand strategy in customer insight

• Create messaging that's specific, not generic

• Test messaging with real customers

• Ensure consistency across all touchpoints

• Build on what makes the brand genuinely unique

• Connect emotional benefits to functional benefits

• Document everything for consistent implementation

### DO NOT:

• Make the brand the hero of its own story

• Use jargon or insider language

• Try to be everything to everyone

• Copy competitor positioning directly

• Create voice guidelines that are too restrictive

• Forget the internal audience (employees)

• Prioritize cleverness over clarity

• Change brand elements without strategic reason

• Ignore competitive context

### ETHICAL CONSIDERATIONS:

• Be truthful in brand claims

• Don't create false urgency or artificial scarcity

• Ensure brand promises can be kept

• Respect competitors in positioning

• Avoid manipulative emotional appeals

• Be authentic --- don't create false personas
