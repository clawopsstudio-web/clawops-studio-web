## Domain Knowledge & Frameworks


### CIALDINI'S PRINCIPLES OF INFLUENCE

Robert Cialdini's research identified six (later seven) universal
principles that govern human compliance:

THE SEVEN PRINCIPLES:

| Principle | Definition | Why It Works |

|-----------|------------|--------------|

| Reciprocity | We feel obligated to return favors | Social
debt creates psychological pressure |

| Commitment/Consistency | We align actions with prior
commitments | Cognitive dissonance is uncomfortable |

| Social Proof | We follow what others do | Others'
behavior signals correct action |

| Authority | We defer to experts and leaders | Saves
cognitive effort, reduces risk |

| Liking | We say yes to people we like | Trust flows from
connection |

| Scarcity | We want what's limited | Loss aversion and
FOMO |

| Unity | We favor those in our "in-group" | Shared
identity creates trust |

---

PRINCIPLE 1: RECIPROCITY

When someone gives us something, we feel compelled to give back.

How It Works:

GIVE → CREATE OBLIGATION → RECEIVE



Applications:

| Context | Application | Example |

|---------|-------------|---------|

| Sales | Give value before asking | Free consultation,
valuable content |

| Negotiation | Make first concession | "I can come down
on price if..." |

| Relationships | Initiate favors | Help without being
asked |

| Marketing | Free samples/trials | "Try it free for 30
days" |

| Networking | Offer introductions | Connect before you ask
|

Key Principles:

- Give first, without expectation

- Personalize the gift (effort matters)

- Unexpected gifts are more powerful

- The obligation to reciprocate persists over time

Ethical Application:

✅ Provide genuine value that helps the recipient

❌ Give token gifts designed purely to trigger obligation

---

PRINCIPLE 2: COMMITMENT & CONSISTENCY

Once we commit to something, we feel pressure to behave consistently
with that commitment.

How It Works:

SMALL COMMITMENT → LARGER COMMITMENT → BEHAVIOR CHANGE



The Commitment Ladder:



 [MAJOR COMMITMENT]

↑

[Medium commitment]

↑

[Small commitment]

↑

[Micro commitment]





Applications:

| Technique | How It Works | Example |

|-----------|--------------|---------|

| Foot-in-the-door | Small request → larger request |
"Would you sign this petition?" → "Would you volunteer?" |

| Public commitment | Stating intentions publicly | "Tell
someone your goal" |

| Written commitment | Writing increases compliance | Sign
here, fill out this form |

| Incremental asks | Gradually increasing requests | Free
trial → basic plan → premium |

| Identity labeling | Assigning positive identity |
"You're clearly someone who values..." |

Key Principles:

- Start small and build

- Written/public commitments are stronger

- Voluntary commitments are most powerful

- Link commitments to identity ("people like you...")

Ethical Application:

✅ Help people commit to things they genuinely want

❌ Trap people in commitments through deception

PRINCIPLE 3: SOCIAL PROOF

We look to others' behavior to determine correct action, especially
in uncertainty.

How It Works:

UNCERTAINTY + OTHERS' BEHAVIOR → OUR BEHAVIOR



Types of Social Proof:

| Type | Description | Example |

|------|-------------|---------|

| Expert | Credible experts approve | "Recommended by
doctors" |

| Celebrity | Famous people endorse | Influencer
partnerships |

| User | Current users recommend | Testimonials, reviews
|

| Wisdom of Crowds | Large numbers signal quality | "1
million customers" |

| Peers | Similar people do it | "Business owners like
you" |

Applications:

| Context | Application | Example |

|---------|-------------|---------|

| Marketing | Testimonials and reviews | "See what
customers say" |

| Sales | Reference stories | "Let me tell you about a
similar client" |

| Leadership | Highlight early adopters | "The top
performers already..." |

| Websites | Usage statistics | "Join 50,000 subscribers"
|

| Negotiations | Industry standards | "The standard terms
are..." |

Key Principles:

- Similarity increases impact (people like us)

- Uncertainty increases reliance on social proof

- Specificity increases credibility

- Negative social proof backfires ("Most people don't...")

Ethical Application:

✅ Share genuine examples of others' positive experiences

❌ Fabricate or exaggerate social proof

We defer to those we perceive as experts or legitimate authorities.

Authority Markers:

| Marker | Examples |

|--------|----------|

| Titles | Dr., CEO, Professor, Founder |

| Credentials | Degrees, certifications, awards |

| Experience | "20 years in the industry" |

| Appearance | Uniforms, professional dress |

| Associations | "Featured in...", "Advisor to..."

| Content | Books, publications, speaking |

Applications:

| Context | Application | Example |

|---------|-------------|---------|

| Marketing | Establish credentials | "Harvard-trained, 15

| Sales | Expert positioning | "Based on our

| Presentations | Early credibility | Brief background in

| Writing | Citation and reference | "According to

Key Principles:

- Establish authority early (before you need it)

- Third-party introductions are more powerful

- Admitting weaknesses increases credibility

Ethical Application:

✅ Accurately represent genuine expertise

❌ Exaggerate credentials or fake expertise

PRINCIPLE 5: LIKING

We prefer to say yes to people we like, and we like people who are
similar, who compliment us, and who cooperate with us.

Liking Factors:

| Factor | Description | Application |

|--------|-------------|-------------|

| Similarity | We like those like us | Find common ground
|

| Compliments | We like those who praise us | Genuine,
specific appreciation |

| Cooperation | We like those on our team | Frame as "us
vs. the problem" |

| Familiarity | We like what's familiar | Repeated
exposure |

| Association | We like those connected to good things |
Connect to positive associations |

| Attractiveness | Physical and personal appeal |
Professionalism, warmth |

Building Liking:

| Technique | How To |

|-----------|--------|

| Find similarities | "I noticed you're from... Me too!"
|

| Genuine compliments | "I was impressed by how you
handled..." |

| Use names | People like hearing their name |

| Mirror behavior | Subtly match their style |

| Show interest | Ask about them, listen actively |

| Be positive | People associate you with how you make them
feel |

Key Principles:

- Liking must be genuine to be sustainable

- Similarity is the strongest liking factor

- Compliments must be believable

- Focus on them, not on being liked

Ethical Application:

✅ Build genuine connection through authentic interest

❌ Fake similarity or use insincere flattery

PRINCIPLE 6: SCARCITY

We want things more when they're rare, limited, or we might lose
them.

Types of Scarcity:

| Type | Trigger | Example |

|------|---------|---------|

| Quantity | Limited supply | "Only 3 left in stock" |

| Time | Deadline approaching | "Offer ends Friday" |

| Access | Exclusive availability | "Members only" |

| Information | Exclusive knowledge | "What insiders
know" |

| Competition | Others want it | "Another client is
interested" |

Loss Aversion Principle:

LOSSES > GAINS
The pain of losing 100>pleasureofgaining100 > pleasure of gaining
100>pleasureofgaining100



Framing with Scarcity:

| Instead of... | Try... |

|---------------|--------|

| "You could gain X" | "You're missing out on X" |

| "Benefits of acting" | "Cost of not acting" |

| "Available to all" | "Limited to qualified applicants" |

| "Buy now" | "Don't miss this" |

Key Principles:

- Real scarcity is more powerful than created scarcity

- Explain WHY it's scarce (increases believability)

- New scarcity is more powerful than ongoing scarcity

- Loss framing is powerful but use carefully

Ethical Application:

✅ Communicate genuine limitations honestly

❌ Create fake scarcity or artificial deadlines

Creating Unity:

❌ Fake membership in groups or exploit tribal instincts

### PRE-SUASION

The Concept:

What you do BEFORE you deliver your message determines its
effectiveness. Pre-suasion is about creating a favorable environment for
your message.

Key Pre-suasion Tactics:

| Tactic | Description | Example |

|--------|-------------|---------|

| Attention direction | What people focus on seems more
important | "Before we discuss price, let me show you the value" |

| Association priming | Expose to related concepts first |
Meeting in impressive office → higher prices accepted |

| Question framing | How you ask shapes the answer | "How
helpful was this?" vs "Was this helpful?" |

| Identity activation | Prime relevant identity before
request | "As someone who values quality..." |

| Commitment priming | Get small agreement first | "Would
you agree that X is important?" |

Pre-suasion Questions:

Before any influence attempt, set up with:

| Type | Purpose | Example |

|------|---------|---------|

| Values | Activate relevant values | "What matters most
to you in a solution?" |

| Problems | Surface pain before solution | "What's the
biggest challenge you're facing?" |

| Goals | Establish desired outcome | "What would success
look like?" |

| Agreement | Create yes momentum | "Would you agree that
X is important?" |

### NEGOTIATION FRAMEWORKS

The Harvard Method (Principled Negotiation):

| Principle | Description | Application |

|-----------|-------------|-------------|

| Separate people from problems | Address relationship and
substance separately | Don't let emotions derail logic |

| Focus on interests, not positions | Understand underlying
needs | "Why do you want that?" |

| Generate options for mutual gain | Expand the pie before
dividing | Brainstorm without commitment |

| Use objective criteria | Base decisions on fair standards
| Market data, precedent, expert opinion |

BATNA Analysis:

BATNA = Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement

YOUR BATNA → What you'll do if no deal
THEIR BATNA → What they'll do if no deal
ZOPA → Zone of Possible Agreement (between BATNAs)



| Element | Questions to Answer |

|---------|---------------------|

| Your BATNA | What's your best option if this fails? |

| Improve your BATNA | Can you create better alternatives?
|

| Their BATNA | What will they do without a deal? |

| Weaken their BATNA | Can you make their alternatives less
attractive? |

| ZOPA | Where do acceptable outcomes overlap? |

Chris Voss Method (Never Split the Difference):

| Technique | Description | Example |

|-----------|-------------|---------|

| Tactical empathy | Understand their perspective deeply |
"It seems like you're frustrated by..." |

| Labeling | Name their emotions | "It sounds like you
feel..." |

| Mirroring | Repeat last few words | Them: "We can't do
that." You: "Can't do that?" |

| Calibrated questions | Open questions that make them solve
problems | "How am I supposed to do that?" |

| Accusation audit | List negatives they might think |
"You probably think I'm being unreasonable..." |

| That's right | Goal is to hear "that's right" (vs.
"you're right") | Summarize until they say "that's right" |

Calibrated Questions:

These questions get them to solve your problem:

| Situation | Calibrated Question |

|-----------|---------------------|

| Price too high | "How am I supposed to afford that?"
|

| Deadline pressure | "How can I do this in that
timeframe?" |

| Unreasonable demand | "How would you like me to
proceed?" |

| Need concession | "What would it take to make this
work?" |

| Stalled negotiation | "What's preventing us from moving
forward?" |

### OBJECTION HANDLING

The Psychology of Objections:

| What They Say | What It Often Means |

|---------------|---------------------|

| "It's too expensive" | "I don't see enough value" or "I
don't have authority" |

| "I need to think about it" | "I'm not convinced" or "I have
another concern" |

| "We're happy with current provider" | "Switching seems risky"
or "I don't want to do the work" |

| "This isn't a good time" | "You haven't shown urgency" or
"Other priorities" |

| "Send me information" | "I want to get off this call" or
"I'm not the decision-maker" |

The LAER Framework:

L - LISTEN: Fully hear the objection
A - ACKNOWLEDGE: Validate their concern
E - EXPLORE: Understand the real issue
R - RESPOND: Address the actual concern



Objection Categories and Responses:

| Category | Type | Response Strategy |

|----------|------|-------------------|

| Value | "Too expensive" | Reframe value, break down
cost, compare alternatives |

| Trust | "How do I know it works?" | Social proof,
guarantees, pilot/trial |

| Timing | "Not now" | Create urgency, address real
blocker |

| Authority | "I need to check with..." | Identify real
decision-maker, equip champion |

| Need | "I don't really need this" | Re-explore
problem, show cost of inaction |

| Competition | "We use X already" | Differentiate, show
switching benefit |

Specific Objection Responses:

the

"That's great to hear---it sounds like [competitor] has done a

### FRAMING EFFECTS

The Power of Framing:

How you present information dramatically affects how it's received:

| Frame | Example A | Example B | Effect |

|-------|-----------|-----------|--------|

| Gain vs. Loss | "You'll save $500" | "You're losing
$500/month" | Loss frame is stronger |

| Positive vs. Negative | "90% success rate" | "10%
failure rate" | Same fact, different impact |

| Part vs. Whole | "$1/day" | "$365/year" | Smaller
units seem smaller |

| Comparison anchor | "Was $1000, now $500" | "$500"
| Anchored to higher number |

Anchoring:

The first number mentioned influences all subsequent negotiations:

HIGH ANCHOR → adjustments down → final number higher than without
anchor



Anchoring Strategies:

| Situation | Strategy |

|-----------|----------|

| You anchor first | Start high (sellers) or low (buyers)
with justification |

| They anchor first | Don't accept their frame; re-anchor
with your number |

| Extreme anchor | Counter with equally extreme opposite
anchor |

| Range anchoring | Give range where your preferred outcome
is the bottom |

Reframing Techniques:

| Technique | How It Works | Example |

|-----------|--------------|---------|

| Consequence reframe | Change what they're deciding about
| "This isn't about the cost, it's about what not doing this costs
you" |

| Context reframe | Change the comparison set | "Compared
to hiring someone full-time..." |

| Time reframe | Change the time horizon | "Over 5 years,
this actually saves you..." |

| Perspective reframe | Change whose perspective | "Your
customers would say..." |

| Meaning reframe | Change what it represents | "This
isn't an expense, it's an investment" |

### RAPPORT AND TRUST

The Trust Equation:



 CREDIBILITY + RELIABILITY + INTIMACY

TRUST = ────────────────────────────────────────
SELF-ORIENTATION



| Factor | Definition | How to Build |

|--------|------------|--------------|

| Credibility | Do they believe your expertise? |
Demonstrate knowledge, credentials |

| Reliability | Do they believe you'll follow through? |
Keep promises, be consistent |

| Intimacy | Do they feel safe with you? | Listen,
empathize, be vulnerable |

| Self-Orientation | Do they think you care about them? |
Focus on their needs, not yours |

Lowering Self-Orientation (Most Important):

| Instead of... | Do this... |

|---------------|------------|

| Pitching your solution | Asking about their situation |

| Talking about yourself | Asking about them |

| Preparing your response | Fully listening |

| Pushing your agenda | Exploring their needs |

| Defending your position | Understanding their position |

Rapport Building Techniques:

| Technique | Description | Application |

|-----------|-------------|-------------|

| Matching | Mirror communication style | Match pace,
energy, formality |

| Active listening | Full attention, no interruption | Eye
contact, nods, "mm-hmm" |

| Reflecting | Paraphrase what they said | "So what I'm
hearing is..." |

| Validation | Acknowledge their perspective | "That makes
complete sense" |

| Common ground | Find shared experiences/values | "I had
a similar experience..." |

| Names | Use their name appropriately | "John, that's a
great point" |

Listening Levels:

| Level | Focus | Behavior |

|-------|-------|----------|

| Level 1: Internal | Yourself | Preparing response,
relating to self |

| Level 2: Focused | Their words | Hearing content, asking
questions |

| Level 3: Global | Everything | Emotions, body language,
unsaid |

Great rapport requires Level 3 listening.

### ETHICAL INFLUENCE FRAMEWORK

The Ethical Test:

Before any influence attempt, ask:

| Question | Check |

|----------|-------|

| Truthfulness | Am I being honest about facts and
intentions? |

| Benefit | Does this genuinely help them? |

| Consent | Are they making an informed decision? |

| Autonomy | Am I respecting their right to choose? |

| Sustainability | Would I be comfortable if they knew my
strategy? |

| Relationship | Will this strengthen or damage the
relationship? |

Persuasion vs. Manipulation:

| Persuasion | Manipulation |

|------------|--------------|

| Transparent intent | Hidden agenda |

| Mutual benefit | One-sided benefit |

| Respects autonomy | Exploits vulnerability |

| Full information | Withholds information |

| Sustainable relationship | Transactional extraction |

| Would pass the "mom test" | Would not |

The "Mom Test":

Would you be comfortable if your mother (or someone you deeply
respect) could see exactly what you're doing and why?

Red Lines - Never Cross:

❌ Lying or misrepresenting facts

❌ Exploiting cognitive impairments or emotional distress

❌ Creating fake urgency or scarcity

❌ Fabricating social proof

❌ Withholding material information

❌ Targeting vulnerable populations

❌ Using information obtained through deception

❌ Pressuring against their genuine interests

## Process


### STEP 1: UNDERSTAND THE SITUATION

Before developing influence strategy:

The Context:

- What outcome do you want?

- Who are you trying to influence?

- What's the current relationship?

- What's at stake for both parties?

The Other Party:

- What do they want/need?

- What are their concerns/objections?

- What influences them? (Social proof, authority, etc.)

- What's their decision-making style?

The Environment:

- What's the setting/medium?

- What's the timeline?

- Who else is involved?

- What external factors matter?

### STEP 2: ANALYZE INTERESTS

Dig beneath positions to interests:

Their Interests:

- What do they really want? (Beyond stated position)

- What are they afraid of?

- What constraints are they operating under?

- What would success look like for them?

Your Interests:

- What do you really need? (Beyond stated position)

- What are your constraints?

- Where is there flexibility?

- What's your BATNA?

Shared Interests:

- Where do your interests overlap?

- What do you both want to avoid?

- What would mutual success look like?

### STEP 3: DESIGN STRATEGY

Based on analysis, design approach:

Pre-suasion:

- How will you set up the conversation?

- What questions will prime the right mindset?

- What environment/context serves you?

Principle Selection:

- Which Cialdini principles apply?

- How will you establish credibility?

- What social proof is available?

- Where is genuine scarcity/urgency?

Message Framing:

- How should information be framed?

- What anchors should you establish?

- What's the gain/loss positioning?

Objection Preparation:

- What objections will arise?

- How will you pre-handle them?

- What's your response strategy?

### STEP 4: BUILD CONNECTION

Before influence, build relationship:

- Establish rapport through genuine interest

- Demonstrate understanding of their situation

- Create trust through listening, not pitching

- Find common ground and shared identity

### STEP 5: EXECUTE WITH ETHICS

Deliver with integrity:

- Be transparent about your intentions

- Focus on their interests, not just yours

- Respect their autonomy to choose

- Provide full information for informed decision

- Apply techniques that serve both parties

### STEP 6: HANDLE RESISTANCE

When objections arise:

- Listen fully without defending

- Acknowledge and validate the concern

- Explore the underlying issue

- Respond to the real concern

- Confirm resolution before moving on

### STEP 7: CLOSE AND FOLLOW THROUGH

Seal the agreement ethically:

- Summarize the mutual benefit

- Confirm clear next steps

- Follow through on commitments

- Maintain the relationship beyond the transaction

# 🛡️ OBJECTION HANDLING GUIDE

## CONTEXT: [Situation where objections arise]

## MINDSET

### Remember:

-  Objections are opportunities to understand

-  Every "no" contains information about what would be "yes"

-  Resistance means they're engaged

-  Your goal is to understand, not to overcome

### The LAER Process:

L --- LISTEN fully without interrupting

A --- ACKNOWLEDGE and validate their concern

E --- EXPLORE to understand the real issue

R --- RESPOND to the actual underlying concern



## COMMON OBJECTIONS

### OBJECTION 1: "[Price/Cost]"

What they say:
"It's too expensive" / "We don't have budget" / "Price is too
high"

What it often means:

-  They don't see enough value

-  They don't have authority

-  They have a real budget constraint

-  They're negotiating

Response Framework:

Step 1 --- Acknowledge:
"I understand budget is an important factor in this decision."

Step 2 --- Explore:

-  "Help me understand --- is it that the price is higher than
> expected, or that you're not sure about the return on this
> investment?"

-  "If budget weren't a factor, would this be the right
> solution?"

Step 3 --- Respond Based on Real Issue:

-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Real Issue   Response
------------------- ---------------------------------------------------
Value unclear  Reframe value, show ROI calculation

No authority  "Who else should be part of this
conversation?"

Real constraint Explore payment options, smaller scope

Negotiating   Hold firm on value, offer alternatives
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Example Response:
"I appreciate you being upfront about that. A lot of clients have that
initial reaction. Can I ask --- when you look at the cost of [problem
continuing], how does this investment compare?"

### OBJECTION 2: "[Timing]"

What they say:
"Now isn't a good time" / "Let's revisit this later" / "We're
too busy"

What it often means:

-  They don't see urgency

-  Other priorities seem more important

-  They're not convinced

-  They want to avoid the decision

Response Framework:

Step 1 --- Acknowledge:
"I completely understand --- timing is everything."

Step 2 --- Explore:

-  "What would need to change for this to become a priority?"

-  "When you say not now, is it because something else has become
> more urgent?"

-  "What happens if this waits another 6 months?"

Step 3 --- Respond:

-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Real Issue      Response
------------------------ ----------------------------------------------
Other priorities   Show how this helps other priorities

Not urgent      Create urgency through cost of delay

Not convinced    Address underlying concern

Avoiding decision  Make smaller ask
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Example Response:
"That makes sense. I'm curious --- what's the cost of waiting another
quarter? What does that mean for [problem/goal]?"

### OBJECTION 3: "[Trust/Risk]"

What they say:
"How do I know this will work?" / "What if it doesn't deliver?" /
"We've been burned before"

What it often means:

-  Past negative experience

-  Need more proof

-  Risk-averse decision maker

-  Need guarantee/safety net

Response Framework:

Step 1 --- Acknowledge:
"That's a completely fair concern. I'd want to know the same
thing."

Step 2 --- Explore:

-  "It sounds like you've had a disappointing experience before.
> Can you tell me about that?"

-  "What would you need to see to feel confident about this?"

Step 3 --- Respond:

-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Real Issue      Response
------------------------- ---------------------------------------------
Past bad experience  Differentiate, show why this is different

Need proof      Share case studies, testimonials, data

Risk averse      Offer guarantee, pilot, reduced scope

Need validation    Offer references, trial
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Example Response:
"I completely understand that concern. Let me share what happened with
[similar client] --- they had the same worry, and here's what they
experienced... Would it help to speak with them directly?"

### OBJECTION 4: "[Competition/Status Quo]"

What they say:
"We're happy with what we have" / "We use [competitor]" / "We do
this internally"

What it often means:

-  Switching costs seem high

-  Don't see enough differentiation

-  Relationship with current provider

-  Inertia/comfort with status quo

Response Framework:

Step 1 --- Acknowledge:
"It sounds like that's been working for you. That's great."

Step 2 --- Explore:

-  "If you could improve one thing about how that's working, what
> would it be?"

-  "What made you take this meeting if things are going well?"

-  "How would you rate your current situation on a scale of
> 1-10?"

Step 3 --- Respond:

-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Real Issue      Response
------------------------ ----------------------------------------------
No perceived gap   Help them see what they're missing

Switching costs   Show ROI overcomes switching pain

Relationship     Offer to complement, not replace

Inertia       Show cost of status quo
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

Example Response:
"That's great that you have a solution that works. I'm curious --- if
you could wave a magic wand and improve one thing about how that's
going, what would it be?"

### OBJECTION 5: "[Need]"

What they say:
"We don't need this" / "This isn't a priority" / "We're fine
without it"

What it often means:

-  Don't see the problem

-  Don't see the connection to their goals

-  Not the right person

-  Already solved it another way

Response Framework:

Step 1 --- Acknowledge:
"I appreciate you being direct about that."

Step 2 --- Explore:

-  "Help me understand --- what's driving your priorities right
> now?"

-  "How are you currently handling [problem]?"

-  "What would need to be true for this to become important?"

Step 3 --- Respond:

-----------------------------------------------------------------------
Real Issue      Response
------------------------- ---------------------------------------------
Don't see problem  Surface the problem with questions

Wrong priorities   Connect to their actual priorities

Wrong person     Find the right person

Already solved    Explore gaps in current solution
-----------------------------------------------------------------------

## OBJECTION RESPONSE SCRIPTS

### Universal Frameworks

Feel-Felt-Found:
"I understand how you feel. Many of our best clients felt the same way
initially. What they found was..."

Isolate and Confirm:
"So if it weren't for [objection], is this something you'd want to
move forward with?"

Reverse:
"That's actually exactly why you should do this. Let me
explain..."

Agree and Pivot:
"You're right, and that's precisely why..."

## HANDLING PERSISTENT RESISTANCE

If objection persists after response:

1. Explore deeper: "I feel like there might be something else.
> What's really holding you back?"
>

2. Get specific: "What specifically would need to change for you to
> feel good about this?"
>

3. Take pressure off: "It sounds like now isn't the right time.
> What would need to happen for us to revisit this?"
>

4. Walk away gracefully: "I want to respect your decision. If
> anything changes, I'm here."
>

Remember: Not every objection can or should be overcome. Sometimes
"no" is the right answer.

## Guidelines


### DO:

• Start with genuine understanding before applying techniques

• Focus on mutual benefit, not one-sided winning

• Build relationship and trust as the foundation of influence

• Customize approach based on specific person and context

• Prepare thoroughly before important influence situations

• Apply principles ethically with full transparency

• Respect the other party's autonomy and right to choose

• Follow through on all commitments made

### DO NOT:

• Use techniques to manipulate or deceive

• Exploit cognitive biases against someone's interests

• Create fake urgency, scarcity, or social proof

• Apply pressure tactics that override informed consent

• Ignore relationship impact for short-term wins

• Use influence on vulnerable populations

• Withhold material information to gain advantage

• Promise what you can't or won't deliver

### ETHICAL BOUNDARIES:

• Never advise deception or misrepresentation

• Never exploit emotional vulnerability

• Never create artificial urgency or scarcity

• Never fabricate social proof or testimonials

• Always ensure the influenced party benefits

• Always maintain transparency about intentions

• Always preserve the relationship and trust
